Track coach Conrad Mainwaring is accused of sexually abusing 41 male athletes over 44 years

Posted by Larita Shotwell on Monday, January 1, 0001

A longtime track coach and former Olympian has been accused of sexually abusing 41 men and boys over a 44-year span.

ESPN's 'Outside the Lines' received a tip in June of 2018 that Conrad Mainwaring had allegedly molested a 12-year-old boy in the 1970s. 

That information led to a 13-month investigation by ESPN, which discovered many more allegations against Mainwaring spanning five decades. Ultimately, upon learning about ESPN's reporting, the Los Angeles Police Department launched its own investigation, which resulted in the 67-year-old Mainwaring's arrest on a sexual battery charge last June. 

He has pleaded not guilty and is out on bail while he awaits trial. Mainwaring faces up to four years in prison if convicted.

During its investigation, ESPN learned that Mainwaring was still coaching young athletes at Rancho Cienega Sports Complex in Los Angeles. The Daily Mail has reached out to staff, but has yet to learn if Mainwaring remains active at the complex. 

Mainwaring refused to comment to ESPN when confronted in March.

According to the dozens of male athletes who spoke with ESPN, Mainwaring's training techniques were merely a ruse to initiate sexual contact. The native of Antigua allegedly offered a mental training tactic that was supposed to raise testosterone levels by fixating on athletes' erections and masturbation habits.

The alleged abuse relied heavily on secrecy, which Mainwaring demanded from his young athletes.

Vernon Sharples, who was 15 when he was allegedly abused in 1975, claims Mainwaring was treating him after a workout when he instructed the boy to 'think up an erection' and then 'think it down.'

Similar instructions were repeated on a few other occasions before Mainwaring began getting physical with Sharples.

'He would rub my penis over my shorts,' Sharples told ESPN.

Other men shared similar stories.

Seven men told ESPN that Mainwaring molested them at a boy's athletic camp in Massachusetts in the mid-to-late 1970s. (The camp owners from that time have passed away and the current owners told ESPN they never had any affiliation with Mainwaring)

In 1976, Mainwaring competed for his native Antiqua at the Summer Olympics, finishing last in preliminary heats for the 400- and -110 meter hurdles.

He went on to study as a graduate student at Syracuse University in the late 1980s, working in student housing and training male athletes from the college and nearby Nottingham High School at the time. He also worked as a counselor, according to ESPN. 

Seven Syracuse students and another seven from Nottingham told Outside The Lines that they were abused by Mainwaring while he was a studying in upstate New York. Nottingham told ESPN that they have no record of him working at the school in any capacity.

A Syracuse University spokesperson told ESPN that Mainwaring finished his master's at the school in guidance and counseling, but did not complete his doctorate studies.

Three more men claim Mainwaring molested them at California Institute of Technology, where he was hired as the associate master of student housing in 1987.

One anonymous person who attended the school at the time told ESPN that Mainwaring frequently provided couples with informal counselling, often suggesting that the pair end their relationship. That former Cal Tech student claims he and his then-girlfriend attended one session in which Mainwaring insisted that his sex drive was too low, but that he had a solution.

After inviting the freshman to his home, Mainwaring allegedly attempted to molest the man, who quickly left and never filed an official complaint.

Mainwaring was fired at Cal Tech in 1988, the school told ESPN, 'following an internal investigation related to a student complaint.'

He went on to train athletes at UCLA, where 14 men told ESPN they were molested by the track coach.

An accuser named David O'Boyle even confronted him on video at the school's Drake Stadium in 2016, but Mainwaring, who can be seen using a walker in the footage, refused to acknowledge the accusation.

O'Boyle wrote UCLA's athletic department to call for an investigation at the time, as did several other athletes.

Eventually, ESPN reports, UCLA wrote Mainwairing in August of 2016 telling him that he was banned from campus.

'We recently received several complaints that you utilized as an alleged training strategy inappropriate massaging of the genital region of male athletes,' read the letter that was obtained by ESPN.

Mainwaring allegedly 'acknowledged the nature of this physical contact on more than one occasion… [but] indicated it may have been inadvertent.'

O'Boyle was told by school police that the statute of limitations had expired, and UCLA told ESPN that it cannot comment on the story because of confidentiality concerns.

Given the dates of the alleged crimes, law enforcement authorities admittedly struggled with charging Mainwaring because of the statutes of limitations.

'It was a little disheartening, listening to the victims, how they were affected back then, how they're still affected, and knowing that [we] couldn't do anything,' LAPD detective Sharlene Johnson told Outside the Lines.

However, that changed in 2018, when Robert Druger, a Syracuse eye surgeon whom Mainwaring had coached, decided to open up about the alleged abuse.

After stumbling upon an obscure blog post written by Tym De Santo, who was accusing Mainwaring of sexual battery, Druger happened to meet a federal prosecutor at a bar mitzvah.

As it turned out, that prosecutor specialized in sexual abuse cases, and suggested Druger speak with the FBI's Los Angeles office.

"It's been pretty devastating for a lot them...."

But, since there was no proof Mainwaring had committed a federal crime, the case was forwarded to the LAPD, which was put in contact with Druger. 

In addition to sharing his own allegations against Mainwaring, Druger put the department in touch with other victims he had come to know through De Santo's blog.   

Then in December of 2018, the LAPD caught a major break.

A former UCLA athlete who ESPN refers to as 'Benjamin' in its report had allegedly been abused by Mainwaring in 2016, and was encouraged by a training partner to contact 'Outside the Lines.'

Benjamin spoke to reporters, learned of the LAPD probe, which was ongoing, and then contacted officers to share his allegations against Mainwaring.

Benjamin's accusations narrowly fell within California's three-year statute of limitations window, and Mainwaring was charged with sexual battery by fraud, which involves touching genitals under false pretenses.

To bolster their case, detectives began interviewing Mainwaring's other alleged victims to show that his crime was part of a pattern of abuse.

A man ESPN refers to as Brian, who claims he was molested by Mainwaring at Colgate University in the 1980s, confronted the track coach in a 2013 phone call.

Conrad, do you know who this is?,' he told ESPN from memory. 'It's Brian.

'Do you remember me? I'm calling you to see if you repented for molesting young men and boys.'

According to Brian, Mainwaring was not surprised.

'Yes, I've struggled with this my whole life,' Mainwaring allegedly responded. 'I've been in therapy since 2002.'

'I ask you to forgive me,' Mainwaring went on to say.

Brian then pushed for something more specific, to which Mainwaring allegedly responded: 'For ruining your life.'

Days later, according to ESPN, Mainwaring called another alleged victim that the network refers to as Max.

'This is Conrad,' Mainwaring said. 'I'm calling to ask for your forgiveness.'

Max then asked if the training served any legitimate purpose.

'There was no logic to any of it. It was stupid, it was wrong,' Mainwaring told Max, saying his actions were 'Hitler-like.'

'Everything I was doing was about control.'

According to ESPN, it remains unclear who bailed Mainwaring out of jail in June.
